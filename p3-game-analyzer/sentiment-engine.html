ABOUT THE
PROJECT

Continuem is a B2B game intelligence platform that transforms YouTube creator reaction videos into structured emotional data for game developers. Rather than reading individual comments or reviews, it analyzes what creators say in real time — moment by moment — as they play a game for the first time.

The flagship case study is Poppy Playtime Chapter 5: 21 YouTube creators, 78,988 caption segments, processed into 8,321 thirty-second windows and 57 named game events, each with a full emotional profile.

GLOBE
VISUALIZATION

DRAG TO ORBIT · 57 EVENTS × 7 EMOTIONS

What you're looking at

A 3D cylindrical visualization built entirely in Canvas 2D — no WebGL, no Three.js. The cylinder maps the game's emotional data across three axes simultaneously.

Horizontal ring axis — the 57 game events in chronological order, wrapped around the cylinder. Drag to rotate through the game's narrative arc.

Vertical axis (on ring face) — the 7 non-neutral emotions stacked. Each emotion is color-coded and labeled.

Height / Z-axis — emotional intensity. Taller orbs = stronger signal. The glowing spheres scale in size and brightness with the percentage of creators expressing that emotion at that moment.

How to read a pattern

A "wall" of tall orbs at a single point on the ring marks a high-reactivity event — a moment where creators responded strongly across multiple emotions at once. The most visually dramatic point is bin 94: Tender Moment Shattered, where the Prototype kills Huggy and Kissy, producing 88.1% non-neutral response — the highest in the dataset.

✓  Where it works
Temporal structural validity
Normalizing each creator's absolute playthrough to 0–100% is the system's strongest design decision. A creator who played for 4 hours and one who played for 2 both contribute to the same bin at the same narrative moment — structurally sound even before AI is involved.
High-reactivity event detection
Events like "Tender Moment Shattered" (88.1%) reliably cluster at moments any human observer would flag as emotionally significant. The system correctly identifies where something happens, even when the emotion label is uncertain.
Neutral suppression improves signal
Deleting "neutral" from the emotion scores when any other emotion fires is a hand-engineered prior that prevents the most common category from overwhelming the output. This meaningfully improves the quality of dominant emotion labels.
Secondary emotion threshold
A secondary emotion is only assigned when its score reaches ≥ 60% of the primary. This prevents weak co-occurrences from being reported as meaningful — a conservative, defensible threshold.
✗  Where it degrades
Lexicon collision
Words like "awful," "oh wow," and "oh no" appear in multiple emotion lexicons simultaneously. The assigned emotion depends on total keyword count — meaning a single exclamation can flip the dominant label. This is not an edge case in gaming content.
Gamer register vs. general sentiment
"Insane," "broken," "I'm dead" are context-dependent in gaming vernacular. "This puzzle is insane" (stuck = frustration) and "this boss is insane" (won = awe) are assigned identically. The model cannot distinguish irony, register shift, or performative emotion.
Frustration lexicon inflation
Phrases like "come on," "ugh," "seriously," and "why" are in the frustration lexicon but are used constantly during ordinary gameplay navigation — not just genuine frustration. Frustration is therefore the dominant emotion for a disproportionate number of events.
30-second window is too coarse
A creator who screams in fear for 3 seconds then laughs for 27 produces a "joy"-dominant window — the fear is statistically swamped. The resolution is appropriate for event-level patterns but loses the micro-arc that makes reaction content interesting.

The system has no ground truth. There is no labeled test set, no confusion matrix, no inter-rater reliability score. What can be claimed is construct validity — the patterns found align with what a human observer would expect. That is not the same as accuracy.

META
OBSERVATIONS

Five conceptual frames for thinking about what this project actually is — from a media design perspective.

01 Translation Machine, Not Measurement Device
The system doesn't measure emotion — it translates surface features of speech (specific words) into emotion categories. That translation is a designed artifact, not a discovery. Every lexicon choice, threshold value, and suppression rule is an authorial decision that shapes what the data "says." The heatmaps look like objective data but are better understood as structured interpretations.
02 The Data Source Creates a Specific Subject
YouTube creator reactions are performed affect — creators are aware of the camera, aware of their audience, professionally motivated to be expressive. The "emotions" captured are public emotional performances, not internal states. The system measures broadcast affect, not player psychology. A developer using this data learns how their game plays on camera — commercially meaningful but epistemologically distinct.
03 Event Naming Is Where Interpretation Happens
The 57 event names — "Tender Moment Shattered," "Duration Frustration," "Lily's Friends Retrieval — Party Joy" — are human-authored labels for machine-detected high-reactivity bins. The AI pipeline found where reactivity peaked. The analyst named why. This division of labor is the project's real structure: the computational system is a surveyor; the human is the cartographer.
04 Scale Reveals Pattern — Pattern Is Not Meaning
Processing 8,321 windows across 21 creators simultaneously is what makes this project distinct from watching videos manually. But the interesting outputs still require human interpretation to become actionable. The platform's value is in surfacing pattern quickly enough that interpretation can happen before a game ships — not in automating the interpretation itself.
05 The Visualization IS an Argument
The 3D globe and the heatmap are not neutral representations of data. They make specific claims: that emotion can be mapped spatially, that game experience is a temporal arc, that 21 strangers' reactions constitute something like collective emotional experience. Those claims are contestable. Part of the interest of the project is how visualization design smuggles epistemological assumptions inside aesthetic choices — the dark palette, the glowing orbs, the cylindrical form all suggest scientific authority. That authority is constructed.
The most honest framing: this is a tool for generating hypotheses about how players will feel — not a tool for confirming them. Its value is in narrowing the space of what's worth investigating, not in closing the question.
