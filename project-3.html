SENTIMENT
ENGINE

Tracking Gamer Reactions in Videos-On-Demand (VODS)

I am developing a game intelligence platform that transforms YouTube/Twitch creator game streams into structured emotional data for game developers. The LLM engine analyzes what gamers say in real time as they play a video game for the first time. Game developers are able to get a feel for the collective playing experience — especially receive near real-time game feedback about storytelling, gameplay mechanisms, design and audio.

Section 01

SHOWCASE

The service ingests raw YouTube creator gameplay videos and reaction livestreams, converts time-series transcript data into a standardized timeline, and executes multi-layered sentiment and 8-class emotion mapping.
By evaluating content in precise temporal windows and normalizing across various playtimes, Sentiment Engine surfaces granular player psychology, locates high-reactivity choke points, and provides self-contained visual dashboards and unified data layers for client-side consumption.

3D Emotion Crystal Ball

A 3D globe of floating bubbles, each representing an emotion detected in streamer commentary during gameplay. The globe animates through named game events — one at a time — showing how the emotional mix shifts as the game unfolds.

The Bubbles

Each bubble's color maps to one of 8 emotions. The number of bubbles equals the proportion of streamers expressing that emotion at that moment — the left legend shows the live count (e.g., JOY ×12 = 12 of the 21 streamers registered joy keywords in that event). The top 4 emotions by intensity orbit the outer shell; the lower-ranked 4 float in the inner core.

Overlays & Sentiment

Bottom-center shows the current named game event. Top-right shows the section label, event count (e.g., EVENT 14/57), and net sentiment score. Top-left lists live counts for all 8 emotions. Sentiment ranges roughly from −0.15 to +0.27, computed as (pos − neg) / (pos + neg + neu × 0.5) across all 30-second windows in that event.

Timeline & Controls

The colored bar at the bottom maps the full game into sections — click anywhere to jump to a specific event. Play/Pause animates through all events; Stop resets to event 1; the Speed button cycles 0.5× → 4×. Drag to rotate the globe; scroll to zoom.

The Two Games

Tabs at the bottom switch between Poppy Playtime Ch.5 (21 streamers, 57 events) and She Was 98 (24 streamers, 39 events). PP5 is dominated by frustration and surprise throughout; She Was 98 shows a slower build of fear and unease.

Emotion Heatmap

Every video is sliced into 30-second windows. Each window is scored for sentiment and labeled with a dominant emotion, then grouped by named game event and aggregated across all creators. What you see is the combined emotional response of the full creator group — not any single person.

The x-axis lists every named game event in order; the y-axis lists the 8 emotion categories. Cell brightness = intensity. A 3-pixel cap at the top of a cell marks the single dominant emotion for that event.

Creator Grid

One row per creator, one column per event. Each cell is colored by that creator's dominant emotion and shaded by strength of their mean score. Creator name and overall mean score appear to the left — green indicates net positive across the full game, red indicates net negative.

Sorting & Data Log

Re-sort events by Reactivity (highest non-neutral % first) or by Sentiment (most positive first). Clicking any cell opens a panel showing every creator's score, dominant emotion, window count, and raw transcript samples from that moment — direct, unedited quotes.

PROCESS

01
Ingest
Validate JSON, resolve segments + duration, filter videos under 60s
02
Window
Slice into 30s windows, concatenate + lowercase transcript text
03
Sentiment Score
Keyword net-score → float [−1, +1] + positive / neutral / negative
04
Emotion Label
Each 30s window is assigned a primary emotion from 8 classes (Joy, Surprise, Frustration, Awe, Fear, Sadness, Disgust, Neutral) via lexicon keyword matching. The emotion whose keywords appear most frequently in the window wins the primary label. Neutral is suppressed — if any other emotion fires, neutral is removed from contention, preventing the most common category from swamping the output.
A secondary emotion is assigned only when its keyword score reaches ≥ 60% of the primary score. This threshold prevents weak co-occurrences from being reported as meaningful signals. Windows where no secondary emotion clears the threshold are reported with a single label — a deliberate conservative choice that favors precision over recall.

Normalise
Absolute time → 0–100% progress → bin 0–99. Enables cross-creator comparison.
06

Bin ranges mapped to 57 named game events from config/games.yaml

Extracting What Was Said

For each video, the system retrieves the spoken transcript. Where YouTube's auto-generated captions are available, those are pulled directly — no audio processing required. Where captions aren't available, the audio is downloaded and transcribed locally using OpenAI's Whisper model.

The result is a time-stamped transcript: every word a creator said, with its position in the video.

Each 30-second window is scored using a keyword lexicon. A sample lexicon covers eight emotion classes:

Sentiment Scoring Formula
Score = clamp( (pos − neg) ÷ (pos + neg + (neu × 0.5)) , −1.0, +1.0 )
The neutral weighting denominator (× 0.5) dampens background linguistic filler, ensuring that conversational banter does not artificially compress emotional highs and lows.

POS_THRESH: > +0.15 → classified as positive
NEG_THRESH: < −0.15 → classified as negative
Neutral zone: [−0.15, +0.15] → classified as neutral

✗  WHAT'S NOT
Transcription unreliable
Using only transcribed text as the data source introduces a wider margin of error. YouTube's own auto-captions perform reasonably well; OpenAI Whisper is less consistent. Adding audio and voice data as a parallel input would be highly advantageous — it would open up tone, pacing, and volume as additional dimensions to analyze.
Lexicon collision
Several keywords appear in more than one emotion category. The assigned label depends on surrounding keyword counts, not contextual meaning.
Gaming vernacular
Words like "insane," "broken," and "I'm dead" carry different meanings depending on context (stuck vs. winning). The system does not understand how to distinguish these situations.
Frustration inflation
High-frequency filler phrases ("come on," "ugh," "why") are in the frustration lexicon. Repetition of these words from transcript inflates frustration counts across events where no genuine frustration is present.
Window resolution
A 30-second window is wide enough to absorb short emotional spikes. A 3-second fear reaction followed by 27 seconds of laughter produces a joy-dominant window.
No ground truth
There is no labeled test set or confusion matrix. Performance cannot be formally measured — can only confirm construct validity, meaning detected patterns align with expected narrative moments.

META
OBSERVATIONS

Taking the concept and research to the next level — some thoughts and further brainstorm.

01 Lexicon Schema Not Yet Matured
Lexicons, thresholds, and suppression rules are defined very simply in this initial phase. Different choices produce different outputs.
02 The Nature of the Data Source as Performative
Creators are recording for an audience. The system captures what they say on camera, not what they experience privately.
03 The Visualizations Inflate Interpretation
Representing emotion as color, size, and position treats emotions as discrete and comparable across creators — risks oversimplification and lack of distinction.
More brainstorming
Next-Generation Architecture — LLM Transition

While the current keyword framework provides rapid, low-overhead evaluations, Continuem has audited LLM providers to transition toward deep contextual analysis for weekly transcript batches (~80K tokens per run).
